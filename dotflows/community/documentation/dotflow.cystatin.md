For a patient with elevated creatinine and decreased creatinine-based eGFR, use cystatin C to determine if kidney function is truly impaired or if the creatinine elevation is due to high muscle mass.

Review the patient's labs including serum creatinine, creatinine-based eGFR, cystatin C level, and urine microalbumin/creatinine ratio. Also review any body composition data (such as DXA with RSMI) to assess muscle mass.

Calculate the combined creatinine-cystatin C eGFR using the CKD-EPI 2021 equation. Compare this to the creatinine-only eGFR.

Add an assessment to the note under '# Elevated Creatinine and Decreased eGFR' that includes:

Assessment (written as flowing text, not bullets):

Include the serum creatinine value and creatinine-based eGFR, the cystatin C level and whether it is normal (reference range 0.60-1.00 mg/L), the calculated combined creatinine-cystatin C eGFR, the urine microalbumin/creatinine ratio and whether there is evidence of albuminuria, and a conclusion regarding kidney function.

If the combined creatinine-cystatin C eGFR is ≥60 mL/min/1.73m² AND cystatin C is normal AND there is no albuminuria:

Conclude that the elevated creatinine is likely attributable to high muscle mass (cite RSMI if available) rather than true kidney dysfunction. State that the patient does not meet criteria for CKD.

Plan (written as bullet points):

Routine monitoring of kidney function is recommended.

No further CKD workup is indicated at this time.

Recommend primary care continue to monitor renal function periodically and reassess if clinical status changes.

If the combined creatinine-cystatin C eGFR is <60 mL/min/1.73m² OR cystatin C is elevated OR there is albuminuria:

Conclude that the findings are consistent with true kidney dysfunction and the patient may meet criteria for CKD. Discuss the stage of CKD based on eGFR and albuminuria category per KDIGO guidelines.

Plan (written as bullet points):

Recommend primary care consider repeat serum creatinine, cystatin C, and eGFR testing within 3 months to confirm persistence.

Recommend primary care obtain urinalysis and renal ultrasound if not already done.

Recommend primary care evaluate for underlying causes including hypertension, diabetes, nephrotoxic medications, and other comorbidities.

Recommend primary care optimize blood pressure and glycemic control as indicated.

Recommend primary care consider nephrology referral for further evaluation and co-management, particularly if eGFR <45 mL/min/1.73m², significant albuminuria (≥300 mg/g), rapid decline in eGFR, or unclear etiology.
