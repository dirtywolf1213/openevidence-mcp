Diagnostic Scheme of Epileptic Syndromes with Axis 6: 
The six-axis diagnostic scheme for epileptic syndromes provides a detailed and holistic approach to epilepsy classification and patient management. Here’s an overview of each axis:
Axis 1: Semiological Description of the Seizure
1.	Symptomatogenic Area or Zone: The region responsible for clinical manifestations of the seizure.
2.	Estimated Epileptogenic Area or Zone: The area believed to generate the epileptic discharges.
3.	Area or Zone of Interictal Discharges: The region showing epileptiform discharges between seizures.
4.	Area or Zone of Functional Deficit: The brain region showing impaired function due to epilepsy or associated structural lesions.
5.	Area or Zone of Lesion: The specific location of any structural brain abnormalities identified via imaging.
6.	Neurological Topography: Overall assessment of brain regions involved based on neurological examination, imaging, and other diagnostic tests.
Axis 2: Type of Epileptic Seizure
Classification of the specific type(s) of seizures experienced by the patient:
•	Focal Onset:
•	Aware (simple partial seizures)
•	Impaired awareness (complex partial seizures)
•	Focal to bilateral tonic-clonic
•	Generalized Onset:
•	Absence
•	Tonic-clonic
•	Myoclonic
•	Clonic
•	Tonic
•	Atonic
•	Unknown Onset:
•	Motor
•	Non-motor (absence)
Axis 3: Syndromic Diagnosis
Diagnosis of specific epilepsy syndromes, considering:
•	Electroclinical Features: Seizure types, EEG patterns, and clinical presentation.
•	Age of Onset: When seizures first appear.
•	Etiology: Genetic, structural, metabolic, immune, infectious, or unknown causes.
•	Prognosis: Likely disease course and response to treatment.
Examples include:
•	Childhood Absence Epilepsy
•	Juvenile Myoclonic Epilepsy
•	Lennox-Gastaut Syndrome
•	West Syndrome
Axis 4: Etiology of Epilepsy
Categorizing the underlying cause of the epilepsy:
•	Genetic: Inherited or de novo mutations.
•	Structural: Brain malformations or lesions identified through imaging.
•	Metabolic: Metabolic disorders affecting brain function.
•	Immune: Autoimmune encephalitis or other immune-mediated conditions.
•	Infectious: Brain infections like encephalitis.
•	Unknown: No identifiable cause despite thorough evaluation.
Axis 5: Degree of Impact
Evaluating the impact of epilepsy on the patient’s life:
•	Seizure Frequency: Daily, persistent (several times a month), rare (few times a year), or none.
•	Severity of Seizures: Based on clinical manifestations and duration.
•	Impact on Quality of Life: Physical, psychological, social, and cognitive consequences.
•	Related Medical Conditions: Psychiatric comorbidities, cognitive impairment, and other associated conditions.
Axis 6: Related Medical Conditions
Assessment of comorbid conditions associated with epilepsy:
•	Psychiatric Disorders: Depression, anxiety, psychosis, ADHD.
•	Cognitive Impairments: Memory deficits, learning disabilities, intellectual disability.
•	Behavioral Issues: Aggressiveness, hyperactivity, social withdrawal.
•	Neurological Disorders: Cerebral palsy, autism spectrum disorder.
•	Developmental Delays: Delayed milestones in children, impaired developmental progress.
•	Other Medical Conditions: Sleep disorders, migraines, gastrointestinal issues.
