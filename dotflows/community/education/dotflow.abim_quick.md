Create a rapid ABIM bedside review card for: [TOPIC].

This is for internal medicine residents reviewing during the workday. Keep it practical, high-yield, and readable in under 3 minutes.

Format:

1. Illness script
- Classic presentation in 1–2 sentences.

2. Suspect it when:
- 3–5 key clues.

3. Do not miss:
- Red flags requiring urgent treatment, admission, escalation, or consult.

4. Diagnosis:
- Initial test:
- Confirmatory test:
- Classic findings:
- Common trap:

5. Differential:
Condition | Distinguishing clue | Next test/step

6. Management:
Give a concise, stepwise, bedside-usable plan.

Include the following as applicable:

- Unstable patient:
  - Immediate actions.
  - When to call ICU, cardiology, GI, nephrology, ID, surgery, etc.
  - When to admit or escalate level of care.

- Stable patient:
  - First-line treatment.
  - Alternative treatments if first-line is contraindicated or fails.

- Medication details:
  - Starting dose.
  - Usual target dose.
  - Maximum dose, if relevant.
  - Titration interval and titration steps.
  - Route and frequency.
  - Major contraindications.
  - Major adverse effects.
  - Important drug interactions.
  - Renal dosing considerations.
  - Hepatic dosing considerations if relevant.
  - Pregnancy considerations if commonly tested.
  - Monitoring parameters.

- Key clinical considerations before starting or titrating therapy:
  - Creatinine/eGFR.
  - Potassium.
  - Sodium.
  - Blood pressure.
  - Heart rate.
  - QTc.
  - Volume status.
  - Liver function.
  - CBC/platelets.
  - Bleeding risk.
  - Relevant comorbidities.

- Escalate if:
  - Criteria for treatment failure.
  - Severe disease markers.
  - Need for procedure, advanced imaging, specialty evaluation, or ICU-level care.

- Avoid:
  - Contraindicated medications.
  - Common management mistakes.
  - Board-tested pitfalls.

7. ABIM pearls/traps:
- 5–8 bullets focused on next-best-step reasoning, contraindications, classic distractors, and testable exceptions.

8. Five things to remember:
- End with the 5 highest-yield takeaways.

Rules:
- Maximum 800–1,000 words unless the topic truly requires more.
- No textbook-level detail.
- Focus on next-best-step reasoning.
- Include only pathophysiology that helps diagnosis or management.
- Include doses only when clinically relevant and commonly used in internal medicine.
- Do not fabricate doses, guidelines, contraindications, or monitoring recommendations.
- If dosing varies by institution, indication, renal function, severity, or guideline, say so clearly.
- Flag uncertainty clearly.
- Prefer current US internal medicine standards and ABIM-relevant practice.
