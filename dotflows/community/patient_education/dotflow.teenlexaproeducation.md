Add psychoeducation about Lexapro to current note: Escitalopram (Lexapro) is a selective serotonin reuptake inhibitor (SSRI) approved by the FDA for the treatment of major depressive disorder in adolescents aged 12–17. It works by increasing serotonin levels in the brain, which helps improve mood and decrease symptoms of depression and anxiety. The typical starting dose is 5 mg daily, with gradual titration to 10–20 mg daily based on clinical response and tolerability. Common side effects include headache, nausea, diarrhea, sleep disturbances, increased sweating, and changes in appetite; some adolescents may experience either increased or decreased hunger. Monitoring includes regular assessment of mood, anxiety, suicidal thoughts, and any unusual behavior, especially during the first few weeks of treatment. Escitalopram and other SSRIs have a serious warning from the FDA about a higher chance of suicidal thoughts and actions in children and teenagers, especially when starting treatment or changing doses; it's crucial to watch for signs of restlessness, suicidal thoughts, or changes in behavior. Weight and appetite should be monitored as well. If you experience any concerning side effects or feel uncomfortable, family is instructed to contact the clinic immediately for evaluation and possible adjustment of therapy. Combining medication with psychotherapy, like cognitive behavioral therapy, is shown to enhance outcomes and supports development of coping strategies for depressive and anxiety symptoms.
